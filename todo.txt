The single most time-consuming step after you've created the indexes will be the iterative process of refining the AI's ability to generate high-quality answers.

This is not a computational bottleneck, but an intellectual and experimental one. It's the "human-in-the-loop" phase where you transform your raw data engine into a polished, intelligent product.

The "Last Mile" Problem: From Raw Retrieval to Trusted Insight

Think of it like this: your FAISS index is a brilliant, lightning-fast librarian. You can ask it for books on a topic, and it will instantly give you the 10 most relevant ones from a library of 36 million.

However, your product's job is not to give the user the 10 books. It's to read those 10 books and write a perfect, one-paragraph summary. The time-consuming part is teaching the AI how to be a world-class synthesizer.

This breaks down into three main, iterative tasks that will consume the majority of your development time from here on out:

1. Prompt Engineering (High Effort, High Reward)

    The Task: This is the art of writing the perfect set of instructions to give to the LLM (OpenAI's API) along with the retrieved abstracts. A simple prompt like "Answer the question using this context" will give you mediocre results. A sophisticated prompt will give you brilliant results.

    Why it Takes Time: You will be in a constant loop of:

        Writing a detailed prompt.

        Testing it with 20 different real-world questions.

        Analyzing the failures (e.g., "The AI ignored a key detail," "The citations are wrong," "The tone is too generic").

        Refining the prompt and repeating the process.
        This is where you'll spend most of your time—tuning the "brain" of your service.

2. Refining the Retrieval Strategy

    The Task: Our current plan is to retrieve the top-k most similar abstracts. You will likely find that for some questions, this isn't enough.

    Why it Takes Time: You may need to experiment with more advanced techniques to improve what you feed to the LLM. This could involve:

        Re-ranking: Using a secondary, more sophisticated model to re-rank the initial search results for better relevance.

        Chunking Strategy: Discovering that your initial "one abstract = one chunk" approach is too coarse and experimenting with splitting abstracts into smaller sentences. This would require re-running your processing pipeline on a sample to build new test indexes.

3. Building the "Defensible" User Experience

    The Task: As we discussed, building the UI to be legally robust is more complex than just printing a block of text.

    Why it Takes Time: You'll need to develop the logic to handle the AI-generated answer, the list of citations, and the extraction of relevant snippets from the source abstracts to display as evidence. This is a more involved front-end and back-end task than it appears at first glance.

In summary: The heavy, automated computation is almost over. The next, and most time-consuming, phase is the hands-on, creative work of turning your powerful index into an intelligent, reliable, and trustworthy product. This is where the real value and competitive advantage will be built.
